Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about savings and investments

What's the difference between saving and investing?
Saving typically involves putting money into cash-based products like savings accounts where your capital is secure and you earn interest. Investing involves putting money into assets like stocks, bonds or funds with the aim of achieving higher returns over the longer term, but with the risk that your investment could fall in value.
How much should I have in savings before I start investing?
It's generally recommended to have 3-6 months' worth of essential expenses in an easy-access savings account as an emergency fund before considering investing. This ensures you have a financial safety net for unexpected costs without needing to access your investments at a potentially unfavorable time.
What is an ISA and how does it work?
An ISA (Individual Savings Account) is a tax-efficient wrapper for your savings or investments. Each tax year (6 April to 5 April), you have an allowance (currently £20,000 for adults) that you can put into ISAs. Any interest, dividends or capital gains earned within an ISA are free from UK tax. There are different types of ISAs including Cash ISAs, Stocks & Shares ISAs, and Junior ISAs.
How do I choose the right investment for me?
The right investment depends on your financial goals, time horizon, and attitude to risk. Consider: How long can you invest for? What are you saving for? How would you feel if your investment fell in value? We offer a risk assessment tool and one-to-one advice to help you choose investments that match your circumstances.
Are my savings and investments protected?
Cash savings with Ulster Bank are protected by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) up to £85,000 per person. Investments are not covered by the FSCS, but we are regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority and must follow strict rules to protect clients' money. Your investments are held separately from the bank's money, so they're protected if the bank were to fail.
