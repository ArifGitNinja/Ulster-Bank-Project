Retirement Planning Guide

Key considerations for planning your retirement at different life stages

20s-30s: Getting Started

Start early to benefit from compound growth. Even small contributions make a big difference over time. Consider taking more investment risk as you have time to recover from market downturns.

  • Start contributing as early as possible
  • Take advantage of employer matching
  • Consider higher-risk investments
  • Review your pension annually

40s-50s: Building Momentum

Your peak earning years. Increase contributions where possible. Consider consolidating old pensions. Start thinking about your retirement goals and how much income you'll need.

  • Increase contributions with salary rises
  • Consolidate previous pension pots
  • Begin to reduce investment risk
  • Get a state pension forecast

50s-60s: Nearing Retirement

Fine-tune your retirement plans. Consider your options for accessing your pension. Think about whether you want to reduce hours gradually or stop work completely.

  • Get professional retirement advice
  • Consider your pension access options
  • Shift to lower-risk investments
  • Plan your retirement budget

Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about pensions and retirement planning

How much should I be saving for retirement?
A common rule of thumb is to contribute half your age as a percentage when you start saving. So if you start at age 30, aim to contribute 15% of your salary (including employer contributions). However, everyone's situation is different. Use our pension calculator to get a personalised estimate based on your retirement goals.
What is tax relief on pensions and how does it work?
Tax relief means the government adds money to your pension contributions. Basic rate taxpayers get 20% tax relief, so for every £80 you contribute, the government adds £20, making £100 in your pension. Higher and additional rate taxpayers can claim further relief through their tax return. There are annual (£60,000) and lifetime (£1,073,100) limits on how much you can contribute with tax relief.
When can I access my pension?
You can currently access your pension from age 55, but this is rising to 57 in 2028. You don't have to stop working to access your pension. You can take up to 25% as a tax-free lump sum and use the rest to provide an income through drawdown or an annuity. There are different rules for some older pension schemes, so check your specific plan details.
Should I transfer my final salary pension?
Final salary (defined benefit) pensions are valuable because they provide a guaranteed income for life. Transferring is irreversible and not suitable for most people. You must take regulated financial advice if your transfer value is over £30,000. We can help you understand the pros and cons and connect you with a specialist advisor if appropriate.
What happens to my pension when I die?
This depends on the type of pension and your age when you die. Generally, if you die before taking your pension, the full value can be passed on tax-free if you're under 75. If you're over 75, your beneficiaries pay income tax on withdrawals. You should complete an expression of wishes form to tell your pension provider who you'd like to benefit. Different rules apply to annuities and final salary pensions.
